## 2 · Landscape — Antoine (Garden Designer)

### Source documents reviewed

* The designer's files label the project **"LIGHTHOUSE"** — another locale alias alongside "Faros," reinforcing the lighthouse/beacon meaning behind the AETHON name.

### 2.1 Garden concept

A layered, drought-tolerant **Mediterranean coastal dry garden** built around sculptural specimen trees and strong vertical structure — designed for privacy and an easy, textural, year-round calm. Stone, gravel and water-wise planting throughout; naturalistic, not lawn.

### 2.2 Planting palette

* **Specimen trees (the signature):** several **mature/sculptural olive trees** — entrance, sea-side, a *bended* form and a *bonsai* — plus **Quercus (oak)**, **Pinus pinea** (umbrella pines, also screening), **Phoenix dactylifera** (date palm 4 m+), **Archontophoenix** palms, **Yucca rostrata**, **papaya**, **frangipani**.
* **Vertical architecture:** **cypress totems** (3.5 m, several) and golden **Swane cypress** — tall slim columns.
* **Hedges / screening:** Eleagnus; mixed Phillyrea + Rhamnus (east) and Phillyrea + Eleagnus (sea side); existing Carissa hedge. Pines screen the neighbour and road.
* **Shrubs \& grasses (texture):** Pistacia lentiscus, Rosmarinus (incl. 'Blue Lagoon'), Santolina, Westringia, Atriplex, Echium, Gaura, Arbutus unedo, Chamaerops, Strelitzia (nicolai + reginae); grasses — Stipa, Pennisetum setaceum, Muhlenbergia.
* **Succulent / "tropical" pocket:** mixed succulents in sea gravel, with yucca and palms.

### 2.3 Hardscape \& features

Dry riverbed; natural rock and **sea pebbles** (graded sizes); irregular natural stepping stones; a **fire-pit** (Lippia ground-cover, stone "kivides"); an **ancient-style amphora**; bridge stones; a **kiosk** screened by pines/cypress; sea-gravel zones. Drip irrigation throughout.

### 2.4 Light in the garden *(ties to the lighting plan + day/night)*

The plan explicitly specifies a **"mature olive tree with lighting"** — the garden is lit at night, with the olive as a lit focal point.

* → Extends **"house of light"** outdoors and supports the **day/night** feature reaching into a garden-at-dusk mood. Confirm detail against the lighting plan (next topic).

### 2.5 What this means for the website *(safe to surface)*

* **Copy:** the garden can carry more than a passing line — sculptural olives, cypress verticals, grasses, a dry riverbed and fire-pit, all turned toward the western sea. (Whether it earns its own section is an open question below.)
* **Olive synergy:** the single accent (olive-green leather) and the garden's olive specimens rhyme — worth leaning on lightly.
* **Positioning:** a screened, private garden reinforces "quiet sanctuary," not a show home.
* **Day / Night:** the lit olive justifies extending the night mood outdoors.
* **Credits:** Landscape — *Antoine (Garden Designer)*.

### 3.1 The lighting idea — the literal "house of light"

* **Warm \& dimmable:** predominantly **3000 K** (warm white), CRI 90, fully **DALI**-controlled (addressable, scene-dimmable) with ON/OFF zones — built for soft, layered, changeable light from day into evening. Some **4000 K** spike spots on planting.
* **Indirect-first interiors:** "Indirect Light (Dali)" is the dominant move room to room — hidden cove/strip sources, low glare. Matches the interior plan's LED-behind-everything.
* This is the real foundation of the brand thesis and the day/night feature.

### 3.2 The garden as a lightscape

* **Specimen uplighting:** Simes "Flower Zoom" / "Flower 35" spikes — including the **lit olive**.
* **Path \& ground:** Kinno / LODES **bollards** in a **"Verde Natura" natural-green** finish (echoes the olive accent); LODES **"Reed 60"** decorative light-stems among the planting.
* **Water \& features:** **LED strips (XL / XL1 rubber 3D)** along the dry-river, rock and pool edges; decorative lanterns; provisions for pendants.
* → Confirms the garden-at-dusk copy already on the site and the lit olive.

### 3.3 What this means for the website *(safe to surface)*

## 6 · Photovoltaic / solar

*Provider: Solvest Ltd / Smart Solar Cyprus (German engineering; Stuttgart-made Luxor panels). Supplier, not a public design credit.*

### 6.1 What's actually being built — three systems

Per Andre, the solar setup is **three separate systems**, not just the one in the proposal:

1. **Grid-tied, net-metering — \~10.35 kW** *(the signed proposal).* 23× Luxor 450 W German bifacial glass-glass panels + Huawei 10 kW hybrid inverter + smart meter; flat-roof mounting. Generation ≈ 177% of consumption (a net energy surplus across the year).
2. **Off-grid system with battery** — stored solar for independence / backup.
3. **Direct DC/AC (hybrid) system** — dedicated to the **pool pump and pool heating**.

→ Net effect: the house can run, store and stay independent on its own sun, and the pool/water warmth is drawn directly from it.

### 6.2 Brand angle — quietly, or not at all (a real judgment call)

* **For a subtle mention:** AETHON is "house of light," built around the western sun. A house that also *lives on* that sun — self-sufficient, independent, the warm water heated by daylight — is genuinely coherent and poetic, and supports the "permanence / belonging" theme.
* **Against:** solar can read as eco-spec or virtue-signalling, which clashes with the museal, understated voice (the Amans of the world don't advertise their panels). Easy to cheapen the restraint.
* **My lean:** *at most* one very quiet, abstract line — the house drawing on the same light it's named for; self-sufficient and unhurried. Never savings/payback/eco-claims, never panel/inverter talk, no roof-panel imagery. Equally valid to keep it entirely internal. Andre to decide.
* **Possible quiet thread:** the spa/pool warmth ("the slow heat of water" already in *The place*) is literally sun-drawn — a coherence we *could* hint at without ever saying "solar-heated pool."

## 8 · Renovation vision

The vision for this seaside home in Cyprus is to create a sanctuary that harmoniously blends Japandi minimalism, Feng Shui balance, and Mediterranean warmth — a space where serenity flows effortlessly through every corner.
Rooted in the principle that “less is more”, the architecture should be guided by human-centric purpose: form follows function, with every line, material, and element intentionally chosen to serve both aesthetics and use.
The home must be ecologically conscious, reflecting a deep respect for its natural surroundings and amplifying the raw beauty of the coastline. Earthy sand tones, marine blues, and water-like reflections will echo the hues of Cyprus, while the use of local, natural materials will ground the space in authenticity.
Designed as an indoor-outdoor living experience, the home should breathe with the climate — windows flung open to invite in the sea air and sunshine. It’s a space built for joy: to savor food, water, weather, and nature, and to offer a sense of ease in daily rituals. The environment must be effortlessly accessible, cat-friendly, dust-resistant and sustain the coastal climate.
While the ground floor is reserved for warm and welcoming hosting spaces, the upper areas should offer private, intimate sanctuaries for silence, meditation, and retreat. Outdoors, the pool and terrace must feel secluded — lush, layered plantings should shield views from neighbors and nearby pathways while still allowing nature to take center stage.
A core design anchor is the daily spectacle of the sun sinking into the sea — this horizon should be framed and celebrated through thoughtful landscaping and terrace design.
The garden is the soul of the property: alive with green as its dominant color, abundant in endemic vegetation, and rich in diversity. It must offer privacy, yet feel open and alive, blending with the rhythms of the seasons. Both garden and terrace should be designed for year-round enjoyment, offering comfort during high-summer heat and even in the occasional rain — always inviting, always alive.
